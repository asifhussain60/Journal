What I Wish Dad Taught Me

To My Children,

I want you to know how this started, because it did not start with a plan or a purpose. It started with a sentence I said to Ishrat one day, in the middle of an ordinary conversation about life. I said, "I wish I had a dad who would have taught me that." And the sentence would not leave me alone.

Not because your grandfather was a bad man. He was not. And not because this is a complaint against him. It is not. Your grandfather did the best he could, as most fathers do. No father is perfect, and no man is handed a manual before he becomes one. I certainly was not handed one either.

What stayed with me was not the accusation inside the sentence, but the absence. The shape of something I had needed and did not have. Not luxury. Not perfection. Just a few clear words at the right time. A few truths spoken plainly enough to save me from learning everything by collision, friction, and disaster.

And once that thought settled, it would not leave. Because I realized something uncomfortable: if I do not write these things down for you, I become the very thing I am describing. A father who had the lessons but did not pass them on.

This is not a memoir written for its own sake. I am not trying to turn my life into literature or build a monument out of memory. That does not help anyone, least of all you. The point is simpler than that, and harder. I want to track the road I have walked, make sense of what it cost me, and leave it behind for you. Not as a performance. Not as a complaint. As a record. As the conversation I owe you.

Because this is not really about the father I wish I had. That would be too easy and too dishonest. Life did not leave me untouched because your grandfather failed some impossible standard. Life taught me what it teaches most men eventually, only I learned too much of it late, through disappointment, bad decisions, wasted years, avoidable pain, and the stubborn arrogance that mistakes confidence for understanding. These pages are not an inventory of your grandparents' failures. They are an account of my lessons. And they belong to you now.

I reached 54 and realized how many things I learned the hard way that might have spared you confusion if I had found the words earlier. Anger. Money. Marriage. Pride. Discipline. Faith. Work. Respect. The price of foolish decisions. The quiet damage caused by not knowing what matters until after you have already paid for not knowing it.

Life has a brutal way of teaching. Sometimes it explains itself gently. More often it takes its payment first and gives the lesson afterward. I do not want that for you. Not if I can help it.

The truth is, even if your grandfather had sat me down as a teenager and explained these things properly, there is a good chance I would not have listened. At that age, I believed I knew everything, and that my parents were simply not of these modern times. Youth has its own religion, and one of its articles of faith is that experience is overrated until your own mistakes begin to corner and shape you.

You may feel the same way reading this. That is your right. But I am writing it anyway.

Because once a man has paid dearly for certain lessons, he should at least have the decency to leave a map behind. And if that man has children, it is not decency. It is duty.

I cannot go back and change what I learned late. I cannot rewrite my own beginnings. But I can leave you something honest. Not polished wisdom from a safe distance, but hard-earned truth from a life that had to find out the long way around. Every chapter that follows is a question I wish my father had answered, and my attempt to answer it for you with whatever clarity fifty-four years of mistakes, searching, and stubborn survival have given me.

Maybe you will not listen now. Maybe you will read this and think your old man is being dramatic. But I know this: the lessons were worth learning, even when they came late. And if they are worth learning, they are worth leaving behind for the people I love most.

Your Baba
