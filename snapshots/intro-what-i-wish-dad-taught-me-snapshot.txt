What I Wish Dad Taught Me

I was talking to my wife, Ishrat, one day in the middle of an ordinary conversation about life, and I said, "I wish I had a dad who would have taught me that." The sentence stayed with me. Not because my father was a bad man, and not because this is a complaint against him. It is not. My father did the best he could, as most fathers do. No father is perfect, and no man is handed a manual before he becomes one.

What stayed with me was not the accusation inside the sentence, but the absence. The shape of something I had needed and did not have. Not luxury. Not perfection. Just a few clear words at the right time. A few truths spoken plainly enough to save me from learning everything by collision, friction, and disaster.

This is what became clear to me as I got older: my goal is not to write memoirs. I am not trying to turn my life into literature or build a monument out of memory. That does not seem to help anyone. The point is simpler than that, and harder. I want to track the road I have walked, make sense of what it cost me, and leave behind whatever I learned along the way. Not as a performance. Not as a complaint. As a record.

Because this is not really about the dad I wish I had. That would be too easy and dishonest. Life did not leave me untouched because my father failed some impossible standard. Life taught me what it teaches most men eventually, only I learned too much of it late, through disappointment, bad decisions, wasted years, avoidable pain, and the stubborn arrogance that mistakes confidence for understanding. These pages are not an inventory of my parents' omissions. They are an account of my lessons.

I reached 54 and realized how many things I learned the hard way that might have helped me if I had understood them earlier. Anger. Money. Marriage. Pride. Discipline. Faith. Work. Respect. The price of foolish decisions. The quiet damage caused by not knowing what matters until after you have already paid for not knowing it. Life has a brutal way of teaching. Sometimes it explains itself gently. More often it takes its payment first and gives the lesson afterward.

The truth is, even if Baba had sat me down as a teenager and explained these things properly, there is a good chance the teenage version of me would not have listened. At that age, I believed I knew everything, and that my parents were simply not of these modern times. With that mindset, I would not have listened to his words, and I certainly would not have absorbed them. Youth has its own religion, and one of its articles of faith is that experience is overrated until your own mistakes begin to corner and shape you.

Still, the thought stayed with me. If the older, more experienced me could sit across from the younger me, what would I say? What would I warn him about? What would I explain before life explained it in a harsher language? That question began to matter more than the original sentence. Because once a man has paid dearly for certain lessons, he should at least have the decency to leave a map behind.

That is what this is for. For the man who finds himself saying, "I wish my dad had taught me that." For the son who grew up and realized too late that confusion becomes habit, and habit becomes character, and character eventually becomes destiny unless something interrupts it. I cannot go back and change what I learned late. I cannot rewrite my own beginnings. But I can leave something honest behind. Not polished wisdom from a distance, but hard-earned truth from a life that had to find out the long way around.

Maybe I would not have listened then. Maybe most young men do not. But I know this now: the lessons were worth learning, even when they came late. And if they are worth learning, they are worth leaving behind.
